Your first media-buying campaign doesn't need to be creative. It needs to be methodical. The goal isn't profit — it's data. Here's a framework that works whether you're promoting a utility app, an e-commerce funnel, or a sweepstakes offer.

## Step 1: Pick two geos and two formats

Don't start with ten countries and five formats. That's not a test — that's a lottery. Pick two geos where you have reason to believe your offer resonates (language match, existing organic traffic, competitor intelligence) and two ad formats that suit your funnel.

A reasonable starting matrix:

| | Popunder | In-Page Push |
|---|---|---|
| **Geo A** | Campaign 1 | Campaign 2 |
| **Geo B** | Campaign 3 | Campaign 4 |

Four campaigns. That's manageable. Each one gets its own budget, its own tracking link, and its own performance data.

## Step 2: Set your budget and timeline

Allocate enough budget per campaign to collect statistically meaningful data. A common rule of thumb: spend at least ten times your target CPA per campaign before making any decisions. If you don't know your target CPA yet, work backward from your offer payout.

Set a calendar reminder for 72 hours after launch. Don't touch anything before then — the optimization engine needs time to learn your traffic patterns.

## Step 3: Define your kill rules before you start

This is the step most beginners skip. Write down the conditions under which you'll pause a campaign *before* you launch it:

- **Kill rule 1:** If a campaign spends twice the target CPA without a single conversion, pause it.
- **Kill rule 2:** If CTR drops below a threshold you set (varies by format), refresh creatives or pause.
- **Kill rule 3:** If one geo outperforms the other by a wide margin after 72 hours, shift budget.

Having rules removes emotion from the process. You're not "failing" when you pause a campaign — you're executing your plan.

## Step 4: Read the data at 72 hours

After three days, open your dashboard and look at four numbers per campaign:

1. **Spend** — Did the campaign deliver? If not, your targeting might be too narrow or your bid too low.
2. **Impressions** — Enough volume to draw conclusions?
3. **Conversions** — Any? How many? At what cost?
4. **eCPA** — Your actual cost per action versus your target.

If a campaign hit your target CPA or came close, it's a candidate for scaling. If it spent budget but produced zero conversions, apply your kill rule. If it barely spent, raise bids or broaden targeting.

## Step 5: Scale winners, kill losers

Scaling doesn't mean multiplying your budget by ten overnight. Increase spend by 30 to 50 percent at a time and monitor for 24 to 48 hours at each new level. If eCPA stays stable, increase again. If it starts drifting, you've found your ceiling for that geo/format pair.

For the campaigns you killed: don't delete the data. It tells you where *not* to spend next time. That's valuable.

## The boring truth

Media buying isn't about finding a secret traffic source or a magic creative. It's about running small, structured tests, reading the results without bias, and reallocating budget toward what works. The plan above isn't exciting. It's reliable. That's the point.
